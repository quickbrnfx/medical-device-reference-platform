# Human Factors Verification

## Overview

Human Factors Verification is the process of producing objective evidence that a system supports its intended users in performing intended tasks safely, effectively, and consistently.

Like all engineering verification activities, Human Factors Verification evaluates the implemented system against documented expectations. It confirms that engineering decisions supporting human interaction have been successfully realized.

Human Factors Verification evaluates whether the system enables users to achieve their intended goals within representative operational contexts.

---

## Purpose

The purpose of Human Factors Verification is to demonstrate that the implemented system supports its intended users and operational workflows.

Effective Human Factors Verification enables engineering teams to:

- Confirm support for intended user tasks.
- Evaluate use-related engineering decisions.
- Demonstrate that usability requirements have been satisfied.
- Identify remaining opportunities for improvement.
- Provide objective evidence supporting stakeholder confidence.

---

## Human Factors Verification Principles

Effective Human Factors Verification should be:

### Requirement Driven

Verification activities should originate from documented User Needs, Human Factors requirements, and system requirements.

### Representative

Verification should reflect the intended users, operational environments, and realistic workflows identified during development.

### Objective

Verification conclusions should be supported by observable and repeatable evidence.

### Traceable

Verification activities should remain traceable to User Research, Task Analysis, Use Scenarios, Usability Engineering, and system requirements.

### Lifecycle Integrated

Human Factors Verification should be planned alongside overall system verification rather than treated as an independent activity performed only at the end of development.

---

## Planning Human Factors Verification

Planning Human Factors Verification typically includes:

1. Review User Needs and Human Factors requirements.
2. Review Task Analysis and Use Scenarios.
3. Define representative user groups.
4. Define representative operating environments.
5. Establish objective acceptance criteria.
6. Execute verification activities.
7. Collect and preserve objective evidence.
8. Document conclusions and resulting engineering actions.

Verification methods may include inspection, analysis, demonstration, testing, or combinations of these methods, depending on the engineering question being answered.

## Reference Example

### Connected Physiological Monitor

A verification activity may evaluate whether a clinician can successfully initiate patient monitoring using the production device under representative clinical conditions.

Objective evidence may include:

- Successful completion of the monitoring workflow.
- Correct interpretation of system status indicators.
- Appropriate recovery from a simulated pairing failure.
- Completion of the workflow within defined performance expectations.

The purpose is not to judge the user, but to determine whether the engineered system adequately supports the intended task.
