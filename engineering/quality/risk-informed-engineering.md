# Risk-Informed Engineering

## Overview

Risk-Informed Engineering is the practice of incorporating risk considerations into engineering decisions throughout system development.

Every engineering decision involves uncertainty. Evaluating potential hazards, failures, vulnerabilities, and operational consequences enables engineering teams to make informed decisions that balance system objectives while reducing unacceptable risk.

Risk should inform engineering decisions rather than replace sound engineering judgment.

---

## Purpose

The purpose of Risk-Informed Engineering is to ensure that engineering decisions appropriately consider the likelihood and consequences of undesirable outcomes.

Applying risk throughout development helps engineering teams to:

- Identify potential system failures.
- Prioritize engineering effort.
- Evaluate alternative solutions.
- Support architectural decisions.
- Improve system resilience.
- Reduce lifecycle risk.

---

## Risk-Informed Engineering Principles

Effective Risk-Informed Engineering follows several guiding principles.

### Proactive

Risk should be considered early and continuously throughout development rather than addressed after implementation.

### Evidence Based

Risk evaluations should be supported by engineering analysis, operational experience, testing, modeling, or other objective evidence whenever practical.

### Proportional

Engineering effort should be proportional to the potential impact of the identified risk.

### Integrated

Risk considerations should be incorporated into requirements, architecture, implementation, and verification rather than treated as an isolated activity.

### Traceable

Significant risk-driven decisions should be documented and linked to the engineering artifacts they influence.

---

## Applying Risk During Development

Risk influences engineering activities throughout the system lifecycle.

Typical applications include:

- Defining system requirements.
- Evaluating architectural alternatives.
- Identifying interface vulnerabilities.
- Prioritizing verification activities.
- Selecting fault detection and recovery strategies.
- Determining appropriate levels of redundancy.
- Supporting cybersecurity and safety analyses.

Risk does not dictate engineering decisions; it provides additional information to support them.
